L'Oréal Skincare: An Honest Assessment of the SOM Growth Question

Our recommended framing for the client team's request on spend levels to move from 4th to 3rd.

The Ask

Can we recommend spending levels to move L'Oréal from 4th to 3rd in skincare SOM?

The client team completed a competitive SOV/SOM audit. L'Oréal currently sits 4th in skincare market share and wants to reach 3rd. They're looking for a spend recommendation to get there.

Before answering that, we need to challenge the premise: is media spend the right lever? SOM rank is driven by multiple factors beyond SOV — pricing strategy, product portfolio, retailer placement, distribution, innovation cadence — and we don't have evidence that media weight is what's holding L'Oréal back.

The ESOV Question

We are not confident in the ESOV rule as a predictive model

The standard ESOV framework (excess share of voice drives share growth) is often cited as a planning heuristic. But the underlying research (Binet & Field, Nielsen) is a cross-category meta-analysis — it describes a general tendency across hundreds of brands and categories, not a predictive formula for a specific brand in a specific market.

Reverse-engineering a spend target from a single elasticity coefficient (e.g., "10pt SOV increase = ~0.5% SOM growth") is not something we should present as a reliable forecast.

The key signal: If L'Oréal already has the highest ESOV in the category (SOV exceeding SOM by ~4pts) and is still sitting at 4th in share, that's actually evidence against the idea that more media spend will close the gap. It suggests the bottleneck is somewhere else — product mix, shelf placement, pricing, distribution, or some combination.

If we deliver a spend number based on the ESOV rule, we risk over-promising a result that media investment alone cannot deliver. The client invests more, share doesn't move as projected, and our credibility takes the hit.

Additional limitations of the ESOV model: It doesn't account for diminishing returns, competitive response, channel mix differences, creative quality, or market timing. A single elasticity applied uniformly across a category this complex is not defensible as a planning tool.

The Bigger Picture

SOM rank is driven by multiple levers — media is only one

Before we anchor on a media spend recommendation, we should be clear-eyed about the full set of factors that determine share position in skincare.

What we can size

Media Investment & Mix

SOV levels, channel allocation, flighting, creative weight. We can quantify media's contribution to share through MMM — but this is one input, not the whole answer.

Beyond media

Pricing & Promotion

Price positioning vs. competitors, promotional depth and frequency, price elasticity by channel and retailer. Often a larger share driver than media in CPG.

Beyond media

Product Portfolio & Innovation

SKU breadth, new product launches, formulation relevance to consumer trends (e.g., clean beauty, dermatological positioning). Category entry points matter.

Beyond media

Distribution & Shelf Placement

Retailer breadth, in-store visibility, shelf space, e-commerce presence. A brand can outspend competitors in media and still lose at the shelf.

We aren't saying media doesn't matter — it does. But recommending a spend level as though it alone will move SOM position is a claim we can't support with confidence. The honest answer is that we need to understand all of these levers before making promises about rank movement.

Our Recommendation

Be honest about what media can and can't do — then offer what we can credibly deliver

Rather than delivering a spend figure that implies a guaranteed SOM outcome, we should frame this honestly: media optimization is one lever among several, and we can help the client understand exactly what that lever can contribute. But we should not overstate it.

What we should avoid

A single spend number based on the ESOV rule, positioned as a path to 3rd place.

Projections that imply media alone will close the gap.

Using a cross-category average as a brand-specific prediction.

Over-promising a SOM outcome we can't guarantee, then losing credibility when it doesn't materialize.

What we should offer

Be upfront: media is one growth lever, but SOM rank depends on pricing, portfolio, distribution, and retail factors too.

Quantify the media lever specifically — channel-level response curves from L'Oréal's own data, showing where reallocation can improve efficiency.

Frame scenarios as "what media can contribute" rather than "what media will guarantee."

Position Omnicom as the partner who gave them a realistic, credible answer — not the one who over-promised.

What We Can Credibly Deliver

Two workstreams, scoped honestly

Competitive Landscape Audit

Map SOV/SOM across skincare by channel and sub-segment.

Quantify the gap to 3rd position.

Identify where competitors are strong on non-media factors (distribution, pricing) vs. where media is the differentiator.

Media Contribution Analysis (MMM)

Build a skincare-specific model to quantify media's actual contribution to share.

Show where reallocation across channels improves efficiency.

Framed as diagnostic ("here's what media can do") — not as a guarantee of SOM movement.

The audit may already be underway. The MMM would be the incremental workstream — but we should scope and position it as a diagnostic tool, not a crystal ball.

Suggested Next Steps

1

Align internally on the honest message: we don't believe media spend alone can guarantee a move from 4th to 3rd, and we should say so directly.

2

Draft a response to the client team framing what we can deliver: a clear picture of media's contribution to SOM, where reallocation improves efficiency, and what the other growth levers look like.

3

If proposing an MMM engagement, scope it as diagnostic — "here's what media can contribute" — not predictive ("spend X to achieve Y share").

4

Consider proactively flagging the non-media factors (pricing, distribution, retail placement) as areas where the client may need other partners or internal workstreams to close the full gap.
